Computer Vision

Computer Vision is to automatically make the computer understand images and videos.

  • Computing properties of the 3D world from visual data (measurement)
  • Algorithms and representations allow a machine to recognise objects, people, scenes and activities (Perception and Interpretation)
  • Algorithms to mine, search and interact with visual data (Search and Organization)

Jitendra Malik, UC Berkeley, gave three R's of computer vision. "The classical problems of computational vision: recontruction, recognition and reorganisation.

  • Reconstruction involves the recover of three-dimensional geometry from images. More boradly it can be interpreted as "inverse graphics". Extimating shape, reflectance, spatial layout and illumination
  • Recognition refers to the prcoess of attaching semantic category labels to objects, scenes, events and activities in images.
  • Reorgranisation refers to grouping/segmentation of visual elements. It is the computer vision analog of perpetual organization from Gestalt psychology.

Computer Vision is the capability of computers to perceive the natural world and extract information from it.
To move within it, to recognise and classify, and to make decisions upon that information and act.

History

  • 1966: Minsky assigns computer vision as a summer undergrad project
    An unlucky intern at MIT AI's lab was assigned "computer vision" as a summer project.
    It turned out not to be so easy - even now academicis stuggle to make eye robots. Yet in the past 10 years we have seen significant progress and computer vision systems are functional and deployable to masses - sometimes for better sometimes for worse.
  • 1970s: Some progress on interpreting selected images
  • 1980s: ANNs come and go. Shift towards geometry and mathematical rigor.
  • 1990s: Face recognition; statistical analysis in vogue.
  • 200s: Broader recognition, large annotated datasets available, video processing starts.
  • 2010s: Deep Learning with ConvNets
  • 2020s: Vision Transformers

What is an Image?

Signal: A (multi-dimensional) function that contains information about a phenomenon.
Signals can be

  • Continuous: Light
  • Discrete: Measurement of a Light
  • Sampling: Reduction of a continuous signal to a discete signal

Any phenomenon: light, heat, gravity and so on.
A 2D image is a sampling of a 2D signal. Note that the 2D signal can also be projection (or slice) of a higher dimensional signal like in MRI or CT scans.
Image stores intensity or "brightness" along x and y dimension.
Video: xy-coordinates + time (Time varying 2D signal)
Image is formed by the eyes or CMOS sensor.
Images are 2D array of intensities for computers.

Epitome

  • Natural images are not arbitrary 2D arrays
  • Edge detection:
